Analyzed the eviction of the Chavez Ravine community in Los Angeles by focusing on the conflict between local residents and the city government. The government used eminent domain to clear the area for the construction of Dodger Stadium, highlighting how the government prioritized economic interests, such as tourism and private development, over the needs of the displaced residents who lived there for generations. We examined the protests in response to the evictions through conflict mapping, an empirical framework, to then evaluate the violent event through three analytical lenses: urbicide, trias violentiae, and life cycle model. By showcasing the struggle against the long-term social and economic consequences of urban redevelopment through frameworks from literature on urban violence, we contributed to the ongoing discussion of displacement and discrimination that are often times faced by low-income, marginalized households in Los Angeles.
